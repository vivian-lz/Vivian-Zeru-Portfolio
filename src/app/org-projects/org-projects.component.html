Wearable Sensor for Health Monitoring

I am in the Du Group Research Lab, run by my PI, Dr. Du. The first project I worked on was to learn how to solder using SMD reflow and a hot air station in order to assemble a PCB with microscopic component sizes. This wearable was created for human health monitoring in order to gain real-life physiological insights. It utilizes EEG and EMG to measure brain activity, ECG to measure cardiovascular data, and NIRS to measure data realted to blood and tissue oxygenation. I am currently working on learning circuit design to hopefully design sensors in the future!

Stormwater Runoff Device

This device was created as a project through Engineers Without Borders at Vanderbilt University in order to help improve the sustainability around campus through being more aware about the aspects of stormwater runoff around campus. This device can be easily re-created in order to serve various purposes related to ensuring that the rainwater that flows throughout Vanderbilt and surrounding areas is as enviornmentally-friendly as possible. Within Engineers Without Borders, we called this project the EPA Rainworks Project since it was submitted to the 2024 EPA Rainworks challenge.
Features

  • Uses 5 sensors to measure pH, conductivity, turbidity, flow rate, and soil moisture of rainwater
  • Utilizes an ESP 8266 ThingDev microcontroller and an Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC)
  • Electronic components are housed on a PCB designed by the Project Manager and myself in EasyEDA (for easy collaboration)
  • Programmed microcontroller using Arduino IDE software
  • Device is powered using 3 1.5 V batteries
  • Utilizes a deep sleep feature to preseve power on the device
  • Enclosure designed in Solidworks and printed out of PLA
  • Device can be placed into the ground, and data is collected wirelessly using VUDevices internet and sent to ThingSpeak channels to graph data

    • My freshman year was spent contributing to this project by helping callibrate the conductivity sensor through salinity and molarity calculations. In addition, I studied the design and electrical processes of the electronics within the device itself. In my sophomore year, I helped lead some of the electrical processes within the device. I helped create the PCB that holds the main electronics (schematic and layout), and I taught other students about this project and how the electronics work, answering questions and delegating tasks to students based on instruction from the Project Manager. I helped debug and callibrate the device, as we made many modifications in order to finish a prototype of the device that could be placed into the ground before the school year ended. Next steps for this project would include making modifications to the code and electronics based on what we discovered with this prototype, and adding in features that may make the device easier to debug when it is in the ground.

    Shoe Drop Device

    For the 2023 TOM (Tikkun Olam Makers Vanderbilt) Annual Make-a-Thon, I was a part of Team 9, where we worked with a PT to help make a device that could help assist in gait training patients to help them gain confidence to walk again. When patients who are paralyzed in one leg are trying to learn how to walk again in their paretic leg, they have trouble applying an appropiate weight to that leg because they do not have much control over that leg. As a result, PT's often try to teach them how to move that leg by telling them to move the leg that they can move, and then help them move their other leg by dragging it on the floor. However, the bottom of the patient's foot needs to be a surface that can easily glide along the floor so that the PT can push their leg for the patient and easily glide their leg across the floor. At first, they were using a tissue box, which is a tempoary solution that may be cheap and effective, but is not reusable and is wasteful. We 3D printed and designed a more effective solution to this issue, which is an easily attachable foot brace that can facilitate functional movement for these patients.
    Features

  • Designed and 3D printed a foot brace using SolidWorks
  • Utilizes two straps made of velcro (one in the back and one to keep foot in place)
  • Includes an extra strap at the top that the PT can use for extra support while assisting the patient in gaining confidence to walk again
